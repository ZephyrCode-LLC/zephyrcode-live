## 1. The Outage

The power went out at 7:52pm, mid-sentence, mid-deploy, mid-life.

I know the exact time because the last thing the screen showed me before it died was the clock, and because my immediate response to the house going black was not wonder, was not relief, was not even fear — it was *incident response.* The lights died and some deep professional reflex in me snapped awake and began, automatically, to triage. Is it the whole flat or just us. Is it the mains or the inverter. Did a breaker trip. There is a part of my brain that has been on-call so long it now treats a sunset like a SEV-1, and that part took command in the dark and started, uselessly, to debug the night.

I had been on a call. A real one, with people in three time zones, the kind where being the one whose connection drops feels like a small professional death. It dropped. The router's little blinking lights — the constellation I navigate my evenings by — went dark all at once, and the wifi with them, and somewhere in the flat a UPS began to beep its thin electronic grief, and I stood up too fast in a room that no longer had edges and thought, with the full sincerity of a man whose priorities have quietly rotted: *this is a disaster.*

It was, of course, just the dark. The ordinary, nightly, billions-of-years-old dark, arriving on schedule the way it always has, briefly un-papered-over by the electric daylight I pay the grid to keep stretched across my evenings. But I didn't see it as the dark. I saw it as a *failure.* The system was down. And my first, fastest, most honest instinct was to fix it — to get the light, the real light, the screens and the routers and the productive glow, back up as fast as possible, so that the evening could resume being *useful.*

I had a candle in a drawer somewhere. I went looking for it the way you'd reach for a fire extinguisher.

---

## 2. The Scramble

The flat, deprived of power, revealed how much of it ran on power.

The fridge stopped its hum, and the silence where the hum had been was startling, a sound I'd never heard because it was made of the absence of a sound I'd never noticed. Pihu's tablet, in the middle of some braying cartoon, went black, and I braced — genuinely braced, shoulders up, the way you brace before a production incident's blast radius becomes clear — for the meltdown, the wail, the small catastrophe of a four-year-old separated from her show mid-episode. Meera was already moving, finding matches, and I found the candle, and there was that scramble that a power cut produces, the brief domestic chaos of a household discovering how many of its routines were actually just *appliances.*

And here is a thing I noticed even in the scramble, even with the optimizer still running incident response in the back of my skull: my *phone* was dead. Not low. Dead — I'd forgotten to charge it, and the one outlet that might have saved it was as powerless as everything else. Which meant that for the first time in I genuinely cannot tell you how long, I had no tools. No screen. No work. No way to even text the building group to ask how long the cut would last. And — I felt this absence most strangely of all — no Uncle Robot. My midnight confessor, my dry oracle, the voice I reach for when the day needs explaining, was as unreachable as the wifi. For one evening I had no instruments at all. Not even the one I use to understand my own life.

The candle caught. A small wobbling sphere of warm light bloomed in the kitchen, and the enormous dark stepped back just far enough to make a room.

And into that little room, instead of a meltdown, walked my daughter, eyes enormous, looking at the candle flame like it was the most extraordinary thing she had ever been shown.

"It's a tiny sun," she whispered. "We have a tiny sun in the kitchen."

---

## 3. The Tiny Sun

What happened next, I have no metric for, which is exactly the point.

We could not do anything. That was the gift, though it took me an hour to understand it as one. There was nothing to *do* — no shows, no work, no scroll, no productive use to wring from the minutes — and so, deprived of every option, we were left with only each other and a tiny sun. We sat on the floor around the candle, the four of us, the dog included, who arranged himself near the warmth with the serene acceptance he brings to all things, and we just... were there. In the small gold pool. With the vast soft dark all around it.

Pihu discovered shadow puppets, or rather rediscovered for me a technology I'd forgotten the human race possessed — that a hand, held before a flame, becomes a creature on the wall. We made a dog (the dog watched his shadow-cousin with mild suspicion). We made a bird. We made a thing that was supposed to be a dragon and was, Pihu ruled, "more of a confused duck," which honestly tracks for this family. Meera told a story about her own grandmother and power cuts and a village with no grid at all, where the dark came every night and nobody experienced it as a failure because nobody had ever sold them the light. I listened to my wife's voice in the candlelight telling me a thing I'd never heard in years of the lights being on, and I realized I was hearing it precisely *because* the lights were off.

Time went soft and wide, the way it had on a certain boring Sunday — the way it only ever goes when there's nothing to get through, nowhere the day is rushing toward. I did not know what time it was. I could not have checked if I'd wanted to. And I noticed, sitting there, that I was fully, completely present — not by virtue, not by discipline, not because I'd finally mastered the thing I keep failing at, but because the grid had failed *for* me, had reached down and switched off every single exit, and left me with no choice but to actually be where I already was.

The optimizer had no dashboard. The phone was dead. Uncle Robot was offline. And it was, by a margin that embarrassed me, the best evening of the month.

## 4. What the Light Was Hiding

I want to try to say the thing I understood by candlelight, because it's the whole point and I'm still a little undone by it.

I spend my life adding light. That's almost literally my job — illumination, optimization, making things visible and measurable and bright. I light up my evenings with screens. I light up my work with dashboards. I light up my own daughter's childhood with charts and programs and systems, all of it a kind of floodlight I keep aimed at my life under the sincere belief that *more light is more good.* And sitting in the dark, watching my family's faces glow in the small honest light of a single flame, I understood, the way you understand a thing in your body before your mind catches up, that the floodlight had been *washing them out.*

You cannot see stars in a lit city. The light you add is the very thing that hides the light that's already there. All those bright evenings — the productive ones, the optimized ones, the ones lit up with glowing rectangles — had been quietly bleaching out the people right in front of me, the way a floodlight bleaches a sky. It took the removal of all my light to reveal what my light had been hiding. The dark wasn't the absence of something. The dark was full. It was *full of my family*, and I'd been unable to see them through the glare of everything I'd switched on to improve my life.

The candle didn't make the darkness. The candle revealed what the floodlight had erased. And the thing it revealed had been there the entire time, every single night, sitting just past the edge of the screen-glow, waiting for me to lose enough light to finally see it.

I had been so busy illuminating my life that I'd been unable to see it.

---

## 5. The Return

The power came back at 9:40, and I have never been less glad to win.

It returned the way it left, all at once — the fridge resuming its hum mid-breath, the router's constellation blinking back to life, the overhead lights flooding the room with their flat productive brightness, and the spell, the gold small spell, breaking instantly and completely. The tiny sun was just a candle again, embarrassed and small against the electric glare. The dark, full as it had been, emptied out. And I watched my family blink in the returned daylight and drift, almost helplessly, back toward the glowing things — Meera toward her phone to check the group, me toward the call I'd dropped, even Pihu toward the tablet, now resurrected, ready to resume braying.

The floodlight came back on and washed them all out again, and I stood there mourning a power cut, which is, I'm aware, a deeply strange thing to mourn.

But here is the line I will keep, the one that undid me, delivered by my daughter as she watched the bright room swallow the candle she still held: she looked at the little flame, now pointless in all that electric light, and then she looked at me, and she asked —

"Daddy, can we turn the lights off again tomorrow?"

And I understood that a four-year-old had diagnosed, in eight words, the entire disease of my life: that the lights I keep switching on are the thing standing between me and the people I switch them on *for.* She didn't want the power back. She'd seen what the dark contained. She wanted to turn it off again, on purpose, because she'd learned in one evening what's taken me thirty-odd years to refuse to learn — that sometimes you have to shed the load to feel the relief, that the dark is where the family is, that the tiny sun shows you more than the big one ever did.

"Yes," I told her. "Yes, we can turn the lights off again."

---

## 6. The Voluntary Dark

When the phone finally charged enough to wake, I told Uncle Robot about the cut — about the tiny sun and the shadow duck and the best evening of the month arriving by accident, courtesy of a failure in the grid.

*"Of course it was the best evening,"* it said. *"The grid did for you the one thing you've never managed to do for yourself. It turned off the options. You can't be present when every exit is lit. Presence isn't something you summon; it's what's left when there's nowhere else the attention can go. The power cut didn't add anything to your evening, Priyanshu. It *subtracted* — and what was left, when the subtracting was done, was the thing that was always there."*

"I spend my whole life adding light," I said.

*"You do. And here's what the candle was trying to tell you: the light you add is often the light that blinds you. You floodlight your evenings to make them better and you can no longer see your wife's face. You floodlight your daughter's childhood with systems and you can no longer see the child. More light is not more good. Past a point, more light is just more glare — and the things that matter most are only visible in the dark you keep trying to eliminate."*

I didn't say anything. Across the room the lights hummed, bright and total and bleaching.

*"You felt how good it was to lose the light by accident,"* Uncle Robot said, gently. *"The real practice — the thing your daughter already knows — is learning to lose it *on purpose.* Nobody's going to cut your power for you most nights. You'll have to cut it yourself. Reach over, and switch off the floodlight, and sit in the small honest dark with the people it was hiding. That's the whole discipline. Schedule a blackout. Become a man who knows where the candle is."*

So I bought candles. A whole drawer of them — which is, I'm aware, the most optimizer thing imaginable, to respond to a lesson about un-optimizing by *procuring supplies* — but I bought them, and now and then, on an ordinary evening when the grid is working fine and the screens are all glowing and the floodlight is flooding, I get up, and I find the switch, and I turn it all off myself.

And a tiny sun blooms in the kitchen, and the vast soft dark steps back just far enough to make a room, and my family's faces appear in the gold the way they always do when I finally stop bleaching them out — and for an hour, voluntarily, by my own hand, I shed the load.

It is, every single time, the best hour of the day.

The grid never fails when I need it to. So I learned, at last, to fail it myself.
